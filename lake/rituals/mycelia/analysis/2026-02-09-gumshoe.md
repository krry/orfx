## Candidate 1 — “Agents become governance surfaces”

### Why unusual
- We’re watching agentic workflows jump from “prompt tool” to *organizational substrate* (multi-agent command centers in Codex; agents embedded in Xcode; agents that understand enterprise governance constraints inside Snowflake).
- In parallel, the security posture is getting specific: “poisoned model” risk is framed in operational, testable signatures. This suggests the market is shifting from vague AI-risk discourse to measurable controls.

### Who is impacted
- **Engineering orgs:** productivity gains collide with change-management, code provenance, and review bottlenecks.
- **Security / GRC / audit:** new asset class to govern (agent behaviors + toolchain model supply chain + agent-generated code).
- **AI vendors / IDEs / data platforms:** differentiation moves from “smarter model” to “safer + governable workflow.”

### Why it may be undetected
- “AI governance” is still framed around *model outputs* (bias, hallucinations) rather than *agent operations* (who/what executed actions on what systems, under what permissions, with what trace).
- Devtool adoption often outpaces policy; shadow usage means organizations don’t yet have an accurate inventory of agentic workflows.

### Key players / likely buyers
- OpenAI (Codex), Apple (Xcode), Anthropic, Snowflake (Cortex), Microsoft (Red Team), plus large enterprises adopting agentic tooling.
- Likely buyers: CISOs, heads of platform engineering, internal developer platforms (IDP) teams, regulated-industry engineering leaders.

### Potential contacts (starting points)
- Product/security leads for: Snowflake Cortex, Microsoft AI Red Team, Apple Developer Relations.
- Analysts covering “agent governance” and model supply chain security.

## Candidate 2 — “Autonomy gets a commission”

### Why unusual
- A rover route (real-world, high-consequence autonomy) gets AI-written commands at the same moment Congress moves to formalize robotics oversight and the White House uses executive action to unwind a chip deal.
- This triangulates: **autonomy (robots) + compute (chips) + governance (commissions / EO)** → the “physical AI stack” is being treated like strategic infrastructure.

### Who is impacted
- Robotics/physical AI startups and incumbents; defense/aerospace supply chains.
- Policy teams and compliance in companies shipping embodied AI.
- Investors: signals that regulatory/political risk becomes first-order for robotics.

### Why it may be undetected
- These events are tracked in separate media silos (space/science, congressional policy, semiconductor deal lawyering).
- Many companies still treat robotics as niche; policy often arrives *before* mainstream commercialization, so it’s ignored until late.

### Key players / likely buyers
- Commerce Dept stakeholders; House sponsors (Obernolte, McClellan, Latta) and any emerging “Partnership for Robotics Competitiveness” style coalitions.
- Aerospace/defense primes; robotics middleware and safety vendors.

## Candidate 3 — “Yield is the policy battleground (and AI is the cost-wedge)”

### Why unusual
- Stablecoin market-structure legislation is stuck on whether ‘rewards/yield’ can be offered — effectively, who is allowed to print value at the payments layer.
- In parallel, on-device models (voice) and agentic tooling compress the cost of customer support, KYC help, and workflow automation, making it easier for fintechs to offer *reward-adjacent* economics without explicitly paying yield.

### Who is impacted
- Stablecoin issuers and banks; payments and neobank product teams.
- Compliance orgs: policy outcomes may define what counts as a “reward” vs “service discount” vs “cashback.”

### Why it may be undetected
- “Yield” looks like a narrow legislative detail, but it’s actually the heart of consumer acquisition economics.
- AI cost compression is treated as a separate tech trend; few analysts model it directly into stablecoin policy scenarios.

### Key players / likely buyers
- White House crypto council stakeholders; banking associations; stablecoin issuers.
- Fintech product leaders looking for compliant incentives.
